The income curves of different career paths after Caltech¶

First post! I will explore using some salary data and crude models, several different career paths that a Caltech undergraduate ideally might take after four years of torture:

  1. Software development job at a large software company (like Google or equivalent)
  2. Software development job at a large software company and then a startup (Snap) at age 30
  3. Investment banking (Goldman)
  4. Consulting (Boston Consulting Group)
  5. Quant hedge fund (Two Sigma)
  6. PhD and then go to a quant hedge fund (2$\sigma$): fake academic
  7. PhD and attempt to become a professor (all at Caltech): true academic

Some Goals¶

  • Include somewhat realistic investment effects?
  • Include family costs
  • Include vacation costs
  • Habit differentiation according to profession

Things I'm missing¶

  • Money value of time: CS jobs will probably work fewer hours than Goldman monkeys, idk how to put a dollar value on that
  • Sudden unfortunate events (health issues, bad investments, etc) - simulations possible
  • Frequent job changes over career would be very weird to implement
  • Accurate career ladder timelines for many of the jobs (SWE, consulting, quant) would make better curves
  • Large purchases (house, car, yacht?), since we don't really know when these will happen. I personally would enjoy living in a clean apartment for the rest of my life, and don't yet see too much of a problem with renting for life.

Some assumptions¶

  • Everyone is quite competent (good enough to get a job at these top-end companies and do well in them)
  • Everybody works until retirement (assume at 65)
  • The non-true academics will have similar eating habits and expenses for most of their life
  • The true academic (option 7) and the fake academic (option 6) will eat free food from campus events and Panda Express during grad school, because they are grad students. This habit will continue into later life for the true academic (option 7) because being a post-doc isn't much better.
  • We will not look at constant career switches.
  • Housing will be \$2500/month for the non-academics until age 30, and then become \$3500/month because they will require more space.
  • For the academics, academic housing will be \$1200 per month, and also go to \$3500/month after age 30.

Base Salaries¶

  1. Google SWE: \$120000
  2. Google SWE \$120000 and senior SWE at Snapchat: \$160000
  3. Papa Goldman: \$74000 analyst monkey (?!)
  4. BCG: \$150000 consultant
  5. Two Sigma: \$150000 quant
  6. Caltech grad student \$32000 and Two Sigma quant researcher: \$175000
  7. Caltech grad student \$32000, post-doc \$50000, assistant prof \$137000, associate prof \$150000, real prof \$210000

Now we can plot some crude wealth curves, without salary growth.¶

  • Cost of housing: as above
  • Cost of food for non-academics: average \$40 per day (forget Google's free food), goes up to \$60 per day after age 30 due to self+kids
  • Cost of food for academics: \$20 per day, goes up to \$35 per day after age 35 if a true academic.
  • Cost of happiness (vacation, non-essential goods) for non-true academics: \$15000 per year, and the fake academic is the same during grad school
  • Cost of vacation is \$40000 a year for true academics after becoming a real prof at age 50. Real profs love vacation.

It will certainly be interesting to look upon this post several years into the future and see how my understanding of the world has changed.

ages = np.arange(22, 65+1)
wages = np.repeat([[120000,120000,74000,150000,150000,32000,32000]], len(ages), axis=0).T

# wage changes:
wages[1][(30-22):len(ages)] = 160000 # snapchat
wages[-2][(28-22):len(ages)] = 175000 # two sigma quant researcher after phd

# the true academic:
wages[-1][(28-22):(35-22)] = 50000
wages[-1][(35-22):(40-22)] = 137000
wages[-1][(40-22):(50-22)] = 150000
wages[-1][(50-22):len(ages)] = 210000

# housing costs
housing = 12*np.repeat([[2500,2500,2500,2500,2500,1200,1200]], len(ages), axis=0).T # annualize
# after age 30:
housing.T[(30-22):len(ages)] = 12*np.repeat([[3500,3500,3500,3500,3500,3500,3500]], 
                                            len(ages)-(30-22), axis=0)
# food costs
food = 365*np.repeat([[40,40,40,40,40,20,20]], len(ages), axis=0).T
food[0:-1].T[(30-22):len(ages)] = 365*np.repeat([[60,60,60,60,60,60]], 
                                            len(ages)-(30-22), axis=0)
food[-1][(35-22):len(ages)] = 365*35 # true academic food
happiness = 15000 # vacations and stuff

# total
net_earnings = wages-housing-food
net_earnings[0:-2] -= happiness # only the academics dont have fun, so no vacation cost
# the fake academic starts taking vacations after grad school
net_earnings[-2][(28-22):len(ages)] -= happiness
net_earnings[-1][(50-22):len(ages)] -= 40000
accumulated = np.cumsum(net_earnings, axis=1)

But that is nonsense, since our Goldman analyst monkey is in debt by age 65, and still an analyst monkey alongside their 22 year old PowerPoint-generating monkey peers...¶

So wage growth and promotions need to be accounted for.

  1. Google has a crazy RPG style leveling scheme, T3 upon undergrad hiring and up to T10 as Google Fellow (like VP). T10 might not be achievable given the size of the company at this time, so let's level cap it at T9 (Senior Director = \$300k). Linear interpolation until age 55 as max level, since we have no clue about the timeline.
  2. I dont think Snap has old people yet, but supposing they continue expanding into a giant (uncertain, given their business model), we can just take some checkpoints for a large company like Google and scale it up for early bird entrance. Same deal as no. 1, just increase the maximum to \$350k at age 50 (as if T10 were reachable).
  3. Goldman has their own ladder according to Quora, with a provided timeline, so we can just use that: 2 years analyst monkey, associate (\$110k) until 5 year mark, VP (\$155k) until nobody knows when - let's say this monkey is MD material and no more - so VP until the 25 year mark, then MD (\$400k) until retirement. By that point the monkey has grown to become a giant chest-pounding gorilla with an ego to go with their size. Salaries from Glassdoor again.
  4. BCG: according to Quora, we have Associate (\$78k) -> Consultant (\$147k) -> Project Leader (\$179k) -> Principal (\$218k) -> Partner (\$329k) as the proper progression, so we need to adjust the BCG salary timeline accordingly. Let's just say it's similar to Goldman's timeline.
  5. Quant: same as below
  6. For the quant hedge fund, I'm not sure if Two Sigma even has people over age 50, so we will take the Senior Management Associate at Bridgewater salary of around \$329k/year as the end goal at age 50. We linearly interpolate between whenever they got the job and age 50 for a constant pay raise.
  7. The true academic is taken care of in the code above: he climbs the academic ladder at only Caltech, as scheduled. He is stuck here his whole life. Note that this ladder concept usually happens over multiple institutions. He will easily have taken more $\int$integrals than naps by the time he retires. Also, he will be a master of applying for grant money and funding and putting his name last on papers his grad students write.

Ah, but wait.. We forgot:

  • Income Taxes: let's set it to 35% for annual incomes over \$150k, and 20% below that. I'm not quite sure how grad student wages are taxed.
  • Equity (especially for the Snapper): let's assume stock futures are worthless there, because they truly are, for now
  • Bonuses, if they are not already included in Glassdoor data: let's assume they are indeed included already
  • Personal investments (quants would throw their money into some strategies): 15% annual returns on accumulated wealth for quants. If they can't beat the S&P500 (approx 12.7% annually for the past 5 years), what are they doing? Also, 10% annually for Goldman monkey. 5% annually for everyone else.
  • Inflation - let's forget about this one, just use 2018 dollars.

wages = np.zeros((7, len(ages)))
# googler
wages[0] = np.interp(ages, [22,55], [120000,300000])
# snapper
wages[1][0:(30-22)] = np.interp(ages[0:(30-22)], [22,55], [120000,300000])
wages[1][(30-22):len(ages)] = np.interp(ages[(30-22):len(ages)], [30,50], [160000,350000])
# goldman gets no constant raise
wages[2][0:2] = 74000
wages[2][2:5] = 110000
wages[2][5:25] = 155000 # VP forever
wages[2][25:] = 400000
# bcg gets same timings
wages[3][0:2] = 78000
wages[3][2:5] = 147000
wages[3][5:15] = 179000
wages[3][15:25] = 218000 # 10 years until promotion to partner
wages[3][25:] = 329000
# quant guy at 2sigma
wages[4] = np.interp(ages, [22,50], [150000,329000])
# fake academic
wages[5][0:6] = 32000
wages[5][6:] = np.interp(ages[6:], [28,50], [175000,329000]) # goes to 2sigma
# the true academic:
wages[-1][0:6] = 32000
wages[-1][(28-22):(35-22)] = 50000
wages[-1][(35-22):(40-22)] = 137000
wages[-1][(40-22):(50-22)] = 150000
wages[-1][(50-22):len(ages)] = 210000

# tax those wages using a boolean mask
bool_hitax = wages>150000
wages = wages*bool_hitax*0.65 + wages*np.invert(bool_hitax)*0.80

# total
net_earnings = wages-housing-food
net_earnings[0:-2] -= happiness # only the academics dont have fun, so no vacation cost
# the fake academic starts taking vacations after grad school
net_earnings[-2][(28-22):len(ages)] -= happiness
net_earnings[-1][(50-22):len(ages)] -= 40000

# to take into account investment, I cannot resist a for loop..
accumulated = np.zeros(wages.shape)
accumulated.T[0] = net_earnings.T[0]
# yes, the fake academic has a strategy in grad school too :)
investment_gains = np.array([5,5,10,5,15,15,5])/100 # percentages
for i in range(1, len(ages)):
    # can't invest negative money
    accumulated.T[i] = accumulated.T[i-1] + net_earnings.T[i] +\
        (accumulated.T[i-1]>0)*accumulated.T[i-1]*investment_gains
